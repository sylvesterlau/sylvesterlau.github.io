“Bubble Trouble”:

Interactive Explanation

Overview

This experimental project is an exploration to visualize the “filter bubble”, which occurs when websites use algorithms selectively presenting information based on our preferences.

The term “filter bubble” has grown into a frequent topic these years. Nearly every products and service we used had embed personalized algorithm and therefore created an echo chamber for us. I build this to get us thinking about our relationship with the online media, and suggest some ways to deal with the “bubble trouble”.

What is filter bubble?

According to Eli Pariser, the inventor of this term, those algorithms create “a unique universe of information for each of us … which fundamentally alters the way we encounter ideas and information.”

Related Project

I was first thinking of designing a product to solve this problem, maybe a news app that shows articles with different views. But some projects with similar ideas already exists. These projects require real users and extensive work in data processing. Another concept design couldn’t solve the problem. But when I showed them to others, it always took them a while to understand. Most of them didn’t have a clear view of what a filter bubble.

“The first step to burst the filter bubble is to know that it exists.”

Insights and Goal

1. Real products require real data. Designing a tool may not be a good practice.

2. We are not familiar with how the “filter bubble” is affecting us, even we have heard of it.

3. Explorable explanations: Nicky Case’s “learning by playing” interactive games pattern seems suitable, considering I was trying to explain a complex theory and make it fun.

So I decided to make an interactive website to visualize and explain how “filter bubble” works: It should give an introduction to this term, and show the way it formulates the content we read, and provide some takeaway suggestions on how to deal with it.

Prototyping

Start from a script

Unlike a product design project, I started with a script from something familiar (like browsing news on the phone) to the introduction and how it changes our perception.

I conducted some test and interview,to find out that the script is too complicated, and the transitions didn’t feel consistent. The interviewees started to describe their storyline and gave me new inspirations.

Finding a simple metaphor

Browsing some other explorable explanations made me realized that instead of trying to cover everything, it should illustrate the shift of one straightforward model. And I explored just the right visual model through paper-based prototype.

The model was simple to understand and remember to the interviewees. And some more complicated rules could be implemented on top of it as “algorithms”.

Step 1.
Choose one piece of information, and move it to “browse history” since you’ve read it.

Step 2.
Increase two picese of the type you read. This is “recommended news” for you.

Step 3.
Move one random piece of other type out of the bubble.

Building the simulation

I tried to learn D3.js to make the simulation demo. D3.js is a JavaScript library for data visualization and provides force layout that I could use to make a smooth bubble chart. The simulation was constructed from basics. It began with merely adding and removing circles which stand for information, and ended with an automatic running bubble chart.

Visual Design

With a suitable prototype, the next step is to build the storyline and visual elements. I choose the combination of a dark background and vibrant colours to highlight different types of news and evoke the visual metaphor of “the universe of information”.

What I Learned

1. First experimental project practice:
There’s a considerable difference between exploration projects and product design. Transferring an idea into script and prototype is a big challenge.

2. The power of a simple concept:
A robust, powerful and memorable idea keeps people’s attention, and it takes efforts to figure out.

3. Coding as a design skill:
I learned a lot about coding to achieve my goal in this project. It gives me confidence and ambition to try data visualization and generative art next time.

4. It’s fun!
Creating something original from the ground up is exciting. But the result is also very unpredictable and hard to control.
